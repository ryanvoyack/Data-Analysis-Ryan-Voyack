Step 1: Preparing the data sets

In looking at the cancer data tables, notice the states (the cases) are ordered by not only by the order variable, but also, by their rate, from highest to lowest. This rate variable is what were interested in, it measures the rate that the instance, death or diagnosis, happened - each out of 100,000 people.

Also, to make sense of the numbers we got for each of the pollutants, well compare the numbers to the guidelines - or limits on the values of each measurement to be enforced so as to promote air quality that is not detrimental to human health - that WHO set for each pollutant. The guideline for particulate matter of 10 microns in diameter or less (which is the type of particulate matter recorded in the data set that we have) is 50 micrograms per cubic meter within a 24 hour mean, or also, 20 micrograms per cubic meter as an average over the course of one year. For nitrogen dioxide, the guideline values are 200 micrograms per cubic meter over the span of one hour, and 40 micrograms per cubic meter as an average that spans one year. http://www.who.int/mediacentre/factsheets/fs313/en/ For lead, WHO did not create guidelines that were as rigorous as the previous, but for the limit of contamination in the air, a decent approximation that they cite is (to keep levels) at or below 0.5 micrograms per cubic meter as a yearly average. http://www.euro.who.int/__data/assets/pdf_file/0005/74732/E71922.pdf (chapter 7.6, page 149 of the document its self, not the PDF - see the table of contents.)

When reading the tables:

  • Refer to the legends to understand what were comparing.

  • Understand that the distance between the two smoothers is trivial, what we are focusing on is their slope, the distance is trivial because the two different kinds of rates are quantified on unrelated scales, I allowed for that in order to be able to graph the relations on the same plot.

  • The black horizontal line (the WHO guideline) and the red smoother (or the pollutant variable smoother) are relative to the same scale however, so when reading: if the smoother is above the hline, then that means the annual mean surpassed the EPA guideline (which is a bad thing for human health), and when it (the smoother) is below, that is more favorable for human health.

Conclusions

With all of this being said, all 6 of my data comparisons have results that are inconclusive; I cannot support my hypothesis that air pollutants can directly describe cancer rates. This is apparent in the plots because all of the plots have one smoother of positive slope, and one of negative slope. Some reasons why my hypothesis is incorrect are: a lot of other things cause lung cancer, like cigarette smoking in particular. The World Health Organization also cites particulate matter, which they deem to be the worst and most dangerous pollutant in our air, primarily causes premature death/disease via inducing heart disesase and stroke in its victims; and only 14% of the deaths that (air pollution) caused were via a contraction of lung cancer.
